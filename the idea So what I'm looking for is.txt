# The playable loop

### Screen layout

```text
        Incoming wall / silhouette cutout
                  ↓
            [ SHARK HOLE ]
              or
            [ PLANE HOLE ]


   Shark bin                          Plane bin
 bottom-left                        bottom-right
```

### Player action

* See the approaching silhouette.
* Decide: **shark** or **plane**.
* Touch the correct bottom corner.
* **Flick** the object toward the oncoming cutout.

### Correct choice

* The object flies into the silhouette.
* It lines up and passes through cleanly.
* **+1 point**
* The wall continues forward, fills the screen, vanishes.
* The next wall is already coming.

### Wrong choice

* **Shark into plane cutout:** shark goes **splat** and slides down.
* **Plane into shark cutout:** plane **explodes** against the wall.
* Lose a life, break combo, or trigger game over depending on the rule set.

# Why this works

## 1. It is immediately understandable

No tutorial essay needed.

A player learns it in about five seconds:

> “That hole is a shark. Throw the shark.”

## 2. The tension comes from speed, not complexity

The design does not need ten mechanics up front.
The walls getting:

* faster,
* closer together,
* more deceptive,
* more visually chaotic,

is already enough to create an addictive skill curve.

## 3. It fits mobile perfectly

The **left shark / right plane** split makes the controls almost bodily:

* shark = left-hand instinct
* plane = right-hand instinct

You are not hunting for buttons. You are reacting.

## 4. The art burden can stay tiny

You are right: this does **not** require expensive animation.

A prototype can work with:

* flat silhouettes,
* simple still images,
* 8-bit or Atari-like sprite treatment,
* one splat image,
* one explosion image,
* a zooming wall illusion.

The game lives or dies on **timing, read clarity, and juice**, not on 3D fidelity.
