SHARK VS PLANE

A flick-and-match arcade game.

Walls come at you — each one has a cutout. Shark or plane. You fling the right one through the hole. That's it. It speeds up until you can't keep up.

That's the whole game. And it's enough.


THE SETUP

Bottom left: a pile of sharks.
Bottom right: a pile of planes.

A wall comes hurtling toward you with a silhouette cutout — shark shape or plane shape. You decide which one it is, grab from the right pile, and fling it.

Right call? The shark or plane flies through the hole clean. Point scored. Wall slides past. Next one's already coming.

Wrong call? Shark hits a plane hole — SPLAT, slides down the wall. Plane hits a shark hole — BOOM, blows up on impact. You lose a life.


THE FEEL

Think Child's Play 2. The factory. Boxes coming down the assembly line one after another after another. That relentless conveyor belt. One wall, then the next, then the next. Rhythmic at first, then the machine speeds up beyond control.

The controls are bodily — shark left, plane right. Left thumb, right thumb. You're not looking for buttons. You're reacting.

The art can stay simple. 8-bit Atari style works. AI-generated stills work. A single splat frame, a single explosion frame. The game lives on timing and tension, not polygon count.

It speeds up. That's the pressure. Walls come faster and faster and faster until you break.


WHERE IT GOES

Different worlds — ice shelves, desert aircraft graveyards, neon military hangars, ocean storms, shark laboratories, midair cloud factories.

Then the hybrids. Pure 80s energy: sharks with wings and gear. Fighter planes with shark faces spray-painted on the nose. Crossbreeds — Shames (shark-based hybrids) and Plarks (plane-based hybrids). Now you're not just matching shapes, you're learning the world's logic.

Sharks are incredible. War planes are incredible. This is where they collide.


THE SPINE

Wall comes → what's the shape → shark or plane → fling the right one → clean pass: score, next wall → wrong: splat or boom, lose a life → faster → repeat until you can't

One ridiculous visual idea. One perfect reaction mechanic. Escalating pressure. Everything else builds on that.

WHY THIS MATTERS / WHAT THIS PROVES

This is a simple game idea from a friend who does graphics and film design. He saw what I built with Federation and said "okay, make me something simple."

What he saw was this:

I run a persistent interstellar civilization simulation on a VPS — 13 Docker containers, Traefik reverse proxy with TLS, full stack. The player leads a star federation through a 100-year timeline. 8 factions with ideology systems. 39 named NPCs with personality matrices, relationship tracking, dialogue trees, corruption arcs, and companion recruitment. 8 rival AI civilizations with personality-driven strategies. A quantum consciousness engine that tracks how different observers interpret the same events differently. A technology tree spanning 6 eras. A quest system. A political engine. Real-time WebSocket updates. Event cards that cascade consequences across every subsystem simultaneously — stability, trust, morale, rights, rival reactions, faction shifts, consciousness metrics, all at once.

The backend is Python/FastAPI. The frontend is vanilla JS — no frameworks, no npm, CDN only. Single HTML files. It's deployed at federation-game.deliberatefederation.cloud right now.

That's what I do. I build systems. Interconnected, persistent, alive systems.

What Federation doesn't have yet — and what I need help with — is the visual side. The simulation engine works. The game loop works. The NPC behavior works. But I'm not a graphics person. I'm not a film person. I'm the engine builder.

So this little shark game is also a bridge. A simple, fun thing that shows I can take a creative idea and make it real in code — and a reason for two people who do very different things to find the overlap.

He makes it look like something. I make it do something. Together, Federation stops being a really impressive terminal and starts being a world you can see and feel.

That's the play.
